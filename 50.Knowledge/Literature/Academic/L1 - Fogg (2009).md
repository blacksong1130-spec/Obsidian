# L1 — Fogg (2009): A Behaviour Model for Persuasive Design

## 📖 Citation (Harvard)
Fogg, B.J. (2009) 'A behavior model for persuasive design', in *Proceedings of the 4th International Conference on Persuasive Technology (Persuasive '09)*. New York: ACM Press.

---

## What / So What / Now What

**What:** BJ Fogg's Behaviour Model (B=MAP) argues that a behaviour occurs only when **Motivation**, **Ability**, and **Prompt** converge simultaneously. If any one element is missing or too weak, the behaviour will not happen — regardless of intent.

**So What:** This model directly explains the failure of dietary tracking apps. They assume high motivation (often absent after the first week), demand high ability (manual logging is cognitively expensive), and provide only weak, delayed prompts (a daily calorie number). B=MAP is the foundational theoretical framework for diagnosing the design gap this project addresses.

**Now What:** Use B=MAP as an evaluation rubric for existing dietary feedback systems, and as a generative framework for designing systems that reduce the ability threshold and increase the immediacy and emotional quality of prompts. Connects directly to [[L5 - Norman (2013)]] on feedback design and [[L9 - Thaler and Sunstein (2008)]] on nudge architecture.

---

## 🔑 Key Conclusions & Design Implications

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Core Model** | B = Motivation × Ability × Prompt (all three must converge) |
| **Key Strength** | Simple, actionable, design-applicable; validated across many domains |
| **Key Limitation** | Underweights social/cultural/emotional context; treats eating as rational behaviour |
| **Why Dietary Apps Fail (B=MAP lens)** | Low Ability (manual logging friction) + Weak Prompt (delayed calorie totals) |
| **Design Opportunity** | Reduce logging friction to near-zero; deliver emotionally resonant, well-timed prompts |
| **Connection to Project** | Every speculative prototype must pass the B=MAP test: is it easy enough? Does it prompt at the right moment? |
