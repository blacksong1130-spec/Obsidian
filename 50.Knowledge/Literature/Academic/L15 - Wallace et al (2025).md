# L15 — Wallace, Koebbel & Heath (2025): Health-Tracking Technologies, Eating Habits and Attitudes

## 📖 Citation (Harvard)
Wallace, T., Koebbel, C. and Heath, J. (2025) 'A systematic search and review focusing on the influence of health-tracking technologies on eating habits and attitudes', *Journal of Health Psychology*, 31(3), pp. 885–898.

> ⚠️ **Note:** Published online 2025; SAGE journal page shows 2026 volume. Marked [待核实] — verify final DOI: https://doi.org/10.1177/13591053251351222

---

## What / So What / Now What

**What:** Wallace and colleagues conduct a systematic search and review of empirical studies examining how health-tracking technologies influence eating habits and attitudes toward food in adult non-clinical populations. Fifteen studies were included. Five recurring themes emerged from the data: (1) **motivators and barriers to use** — why people adopt and abandon tracking; (2) **process of food logging** — how the act of recording food affects attention and behaviour; (3) **reification of data** — how numerical outputs change the meaning of food for users; (4) **disordered eating and compulsive exercise** — a significant sub-population for whom tracking becomes compulsive and harmful; (5) **goal achievement and positive self-appraisal** — cases where tracking produces genuine positive outcomes.

**So What:** This review provides the most current systematic evidence for a central claim in the proposal's Eating Disorder Constraint section: that health-tracking technologies have a documented "dark side" — for a sub-population, tracking shifts from health tool to compulsive behaviour, with measurable negative effects on eating attitudes. Critically, the review also captures the positive cases, giving a balanced empirical picture. The five themes map directly onto the app audit methodology proposed in Section 5b: each theme is an evaluation dimension. The "reification of data" theme is particularly relevant — it describes the mechanism by which numerical food data reshapes how users experience eating, potentially replacing sensory experience with quantification.

**Now What:** Use as the most current systematic evidence for the eating disorder risk section. This paper directly complements [[L6 - Roth et al (2024)]] (which covers weight-related self-monitoring specifically). Wallace adds the tech-specific dimension — how the design and interface of tracking apps shapes the risk. Connects to [[L11 - Kross and Ayduk (2017)]] (language/framing in feedback), [[L3 - Tribole and Resch (2020)]] (intuitive eating as alternative), and the app audit framework in Section 5b.

## 🔑 Five Themes & Design Implications

| Theme | Finding | Design Response |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **Motivators & barriers to use** | Intrinsic motivation sustains use; external pressure undermines it | Design for curiosity, not compliance |
| **Process of food logging** | Act of logging increases food attention — can be positive OR negative | Minimise friction AND cognitive intrusion |
| **Reification of data** | Numbers replace sensory experience; food becomes numerical object | Feedback must restore, not replace, sensory relationship |
| **Disordered eating & compulsive exercise** | Significant sub-population develops compulsive tracking | Build detection + off-ramp mechanisms into design |
| **Goal achievement & positive self-appraisal** | When goal is felt autonomy, outcomes positive | Frame feedback as supporting autonomy, not compliance |

## 💡 Suggested Use in Proposal

> *"Wallace et al.'s (2025) systematic review identifies five mechanisms through which health-tracking technologies reshape eating behaviour and attitudes. Most relevant to this project is their 'reification of data' theme: the process by which numerical food tracking gradually replaces the direct sensory-emotional experience of eating with a quantified surrogate. This is precisely the design failure mode this project seeks to avoid — and the reason the Intuitive Technology scenario prioritises amplifying internal sensing rather than substituting it with metrics."*
