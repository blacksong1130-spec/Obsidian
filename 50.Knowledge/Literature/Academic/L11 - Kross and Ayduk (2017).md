# L11 — Kross & Ayduk (2017): Self-Distancing: Theory, Research, and Current Directions

## 📖 Citation (Harvard)
Kross, E. and Ayduk, O. (2017) 'Self-distancing: Theory, research, and current directions', in *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 55, pp. 81–136.

---

## What / So What / Now What

**What:** Kross and Ayduk present a comprehensive integrative theory of **self-distancing** — the cognitive process of stepping back from one's immediate emotional experience to adopt a more distal, observer-like perspective. When people ruminate on distressing events from a self-immersed ("I") perspective, they often worsen. When they shift to a distanced third-person ("he/she/they") or fly-on-the-wall perspective, they regulate emotions more effectively, reason more clearly, and make better decisions. The research uses experience-sampling, neuroimaging, and behavioural experiments to validate this.

**So What:** Self-distancing provides a precise mechanism for the "observer language" discussed in the Shannon meeting notes. When a user's dietary feedback system speaks to them in a curious, observational tone — "You tend to feel sluggish 90 minutes after high-carb lunches" — rather than a judgmental one ("You overate today"), it is *linguistically enacting self-distancing*. This shifts the user from System 1 emotional reactivity to System 2 analytical engagement. Crucially, this also directly addresses the **eating disorder risk**: judgmental, first-person framing ("I failed today") is precisely the emotional activation that drives disordered eating spirals. Observer framing short-circuits this.

**Now What:** Apply self-distancing as an explicit **design principle for feedback language** across all three 2035 scenarios. The language of feedback — not just the data — is a design variable. Connects directly to [[L7 - Kahneman (2011)]] (System 1/2 regulation), [[L3 - Tribole and Resch (2020)]] (curiosity, not judgment), and [[P9 - Woebot (2017)]] (conversational AI using distanced framing). The Intuitive Technology scenario (Section 5d) is a primary site for this.

## 🔑 Key Concepts & Design Implications

| Concept | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Self-distancing** | Shifting perspective from "I" (immersed) to "he/she/they" or observer view |
| **Self-immersion** | Default mode when processing negative experiences — amplifies emotion, triggers rumination |
| **Mechanism** | Distance reduces emotional intensity, allowing regulated, analytical engagement |
| **Application to feedback language** | "You tend to feel sluggish after X" vs "You failed today" |
| **Eating disorder connection** | Immersed self-criticism → shame spiral → compensatory behaviours. Distanced curiosity → reflection → learning |
| **Design principle** | Feedback tone: 3rd-person observer, not 2nd-person judge. "Interesting pattern" not "you should" |
| **Link to Woebot** | Woebot's CBT-based conversational framing partially operationalises self-distancing |

## 💡 Suggested Use in Proposal

> *"Kross and Ayduk (2017) provide the theoretical basis for the project's most critical design constraint: feedback language. Their research demonstrates that third-person, observer-perspective framing ('you tend to feel tired after these meals') reduces emotional reactivity compared to first-person framing ('I ate badly today'). For dietary feedback systems at elevated risk of triggering disordered eating spirals, this linguistic distinction may be as consequential as the data itself."*
