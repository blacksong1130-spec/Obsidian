# L13 — Rozin et al. (1999): Attitudes to Food and the Role of Food in Life (Cross-Cultural Study)

## 📖 Citation (Harvard)
Rozin, P., Fischler, C., Imada, S., Sarubin, A. and Wrzesniewski, A. (1999) 'Attitudes to food and the role of food in life in the USA, Japan, Flemish Belgium and France: possible implications for the diet–health debate', *Appetite*, 33(2), pp. 163–180.

---

## What / So What / Now What

**What:** Rozin and colleagues conducted a cross-cultural survey of adults and college students in the USA, France, Flemish Belgium, and Japan, examining their attitudes toward food, the degree to which they associate food with health vs. pleasure, their level of food-related anxiety, and their responses to "healthier" modified foods. The headline finding is the **French Paradox of Food Psychology**: French participants showed the strongest association between food and pleasure, the lowest food-related anxiety, and the weakest desire to alter food for health reasons — while also consuming a rich, high-fat diet with relatively low rates of cardiovascular anxiety. Americans showed the inverse pattern: strongest health-food association, highest food anxiety, most likely to pursue dietary modification, and least likely to self-identify as healthy eaters.

**So What:** This study provides the empirical foundation for a claim central to this project: **medicalising food — framing it primarily as a health variable — correlates with higher anxiety and not with better health outcomes or self-perception**. The "Americans" pattern is precisely the pattern that calorie-counting apps reinforce: food as fuel, health as numerical compliance. The "French" pattern — food as pleasure, social experience, cultural ritual — is the pattern associated with lower anxiety and, paradoxically, comparable or better health. This is not an argument against health; it is an argument for the design of dietary feedback that operates in the cultural-pleasure register rather than the medical-compliance register.

**Now What:** Use as empirical support for the "Ambient Social" and "Intuitive Technology" scenarios, where feedback integrates cultural, social, and pleasure dimensions of food rather than reducing it to metrics. Pairs directly with [[L8 - Fischler (1988)]] (food as cultural/identity carrier) and [[L3 - Tribole and Resch (2020)]] (intuitive eating). Also connects to the Itadakimasu discussion: the Japanese and French relationship with food — embedded in ritual and pleasure — is a design model for what dietary feedback could feel like.

---

## 🔑 Key Findings & Design Implications

| Country Pattern | Food Association | Food Anxiety | Health Outcomes |
|-----------------|-----------------|--------------|-----------------|
| 🇺🇸 USA | Health (strongest) | Highest | Poor self-perception despite most modification |
| 🇫🇷 France | Pleasure (strongest) | Lowest | Better cardiometabolic perception despite rich food |
| 🇧🇪 Belgium | Pleasure (moderate) | Low-moderate | — |
| 🇯🇵 Japan | Between USA and France | Moderate | — |

| Design Implication | Detail |
|--------------------|--------|
| **Against medicalisation** | Health-as-compliance framing increases anxiety without improving outcomes |
| **For cultural/pleasure framing** | Food joy is not in opposition to health — it may be protective |
| **Feedback language** | Should not make food feel like a medical exam |
| **Scenario connection** | Ambient Social scenario: feedback embedded in dining ritual, not extracted from it |
| **Eating disorder constraint** | High food anxiety (American pattern) → disordered eating risk. Low food anxiety (French pattern) → protective |

## 💡 Suggested Use in Proposal

> *"Rozin et al.'s (1999) cross-cultural study provides an empirical counter-argument to the dominant dietary feedback paradigm. Americans, who most aggressively medicalise their eating — tracking, modifying, quantifying — show the highest food anxiety and the lowest self-rated healthy eating. French participants, for whom food is primarily pleasure and social ritual, show the lowest anxiety and comparable health perception. This is not a paradox: it is an argument for the proposition that dietary feedback systems must operate in the cultural-pleasure register to be protective, not anxiety-inducing."*
