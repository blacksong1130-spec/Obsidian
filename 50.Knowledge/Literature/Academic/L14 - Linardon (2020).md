# L14 — Linardon (2020): Mindfulness, Acceptance & Compassion Interventions for Eating Disorders

## 📖 Citation (Harvard)
Linardon, J. (2020) 'Can acceptance-, mindfulness-, and compassion-based interventions benefit people with eating disorder psychopathology? A systematic review and meta-analysis', *Behavior Modification*, 44(3), pp. 344–372.

> ⚠️ **Note:** Marked [待核实] in the original proposal — verify full DOI and page numbers before final submission.

## What / So What / Now What

**What:** Linardon conducts a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials testing acceptance-, mindfulness-, and compassion-based interventions (AMC interventions) on eating disorder psychopathology. The review examines whether these "third-wave" behavioural therapies — including MB-EAT, ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy), DBT (Dialectical Behaviour Therapy), and compassion-focused therapy — show measurable reductions in eating disorder symptoms such as binge eating, purging, dietary restraint, and weight-related anxiety. The overall finding is affirmative: AMC interventions demonstrate significant positive effects on eating disorder symptoms, and this effect is most pronounced for acceptance-based and mindfulness-based approaches.

**So What:** This provides empirical support for a key argument in Section 2 of the proposal: that dietary feedback systems designed around **acceptance, curiosity, and non-judgment** (rather than control, compliance, and punishment) are not just ethically preferable — they are **clinically effective**. The project's three design principles ("Amplify, not replace"; "Curiosity, not judgment"; "Scaffold toward autonomy") are operationally congruent with the AMC framework that Linardon shows to be protective. In other words, good design values and clinical evidence point in the same direction.

**Now What:** Use as direct empirical support for the Eating Disorder Constraint argument (Section 2 and Section 5d). Connects to [[L3 - Tribole and Resch (2020)]] (intuitive eating as an acceptance-based framework), [[L12 - Kristeller and Wolever (2011)]] (MB-EAT as the most food-specific AMC intervention), and [[L6 - Roth et al (2024)]] (intensive tracking as a risk factor — Linardon shows what protective alternatives look like).

## 🔑 Key Findings & Design Implications

| Finding | Design Implication |
|---------|-------------------|
| AMC interventions reduce binge eating and dietary restraint | Feedback rooted in curiosity and acceptance has therapeutic precedent |
| Mindfulness-based interventions most effective | Attentional, body-centric feedback aligns with evidence |
| Compassion-focused approaches reduce self-criticism | Feedback tone must avoid activating the self-critical response |
| Third-wave therapies outperform classical behaviour change (reward/punishment) | Implies that reward-punishment feedback architectures (streaks, penalties) are contraindicated |
| Evidence base growing but still limited by RCT design | Speculative design prototypes should be tested against AMC criteria |
