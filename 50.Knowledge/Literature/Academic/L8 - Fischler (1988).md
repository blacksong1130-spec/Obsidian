# L8 — Fischler (1988): Food, Self and Identity

## 📖 Citation (Harvard)
Fischler, C. (1988) 'Food, self and identity', *Social Science Information*, 27(2), pp. 275–292.

---

## What / So What / Now What

**What:** Fischler's classic sociological paper establishes **gastronomy as identity** — the foods we eat become part of who we are (the incorporation principle: *you are what you eat*). He argues that in modern Western societies, the **medicalisation and nutritionalisation** of food — reducing it to nutrients and calories — produces profound alienation from the cultural and social meaning of eating, which he calls **gastro-anomie**.

**So What:** Explains why calorie-counting apps feel alienating to so many users: they strip food of social and cultural meaning and reduce it to numerical data. A successful dietary feedback system must engage with food as **identity and culture**, not merely fuel. This has profound design implications for how feedback is framed and communicated.

**Now What:** Use Fischler's framework to interrogate the social/cultural register of speculative feedback designs. Does this artefact honour food as identity? Does it reinforce gastro-anomie or resist it? Connects to [[L3 - Tribole and Resch (2020)]] on food joy and to [[P1 - Strava (2009)]] on social meaning-making in fitness.

---

## 🔑 Key Concepts & Design Implications

| Fischler Concept | Meaning | Design Implication |
|-----------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Incorporation Principle** | We literally become what we eat — food is self-defining | Feedback must feel personal and identity-affirming, not corrective |
| **Gastro-anomie** | Loss of food meaning in modern medicalised eating | Avoid further reductionism; design for meaning, not just metrics |
| **Food as Social Act** | Eating is celebration, care, heritage, connection | Feedback systems must have a social/relational dimension |
| **Medicalisation Risk** | Reducing food to nutrients alienates eaters | Don't make feedback feel clinical or surveillance-like |
| **Cultural Variation** | Different cultures have different food relationships | One-size-fits-all feedback is culturally inappropriate |

| Overall Assessment | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Strength** | Foundational sociological lens; explains why nutrition design fails at a cultural level |
| **Limitation** | Written in 1988; predates digital health technology |
| **Project Application** | Anti-gastro-anomie = a design principle: feedback should feel more like a shared meal than a lab report |
