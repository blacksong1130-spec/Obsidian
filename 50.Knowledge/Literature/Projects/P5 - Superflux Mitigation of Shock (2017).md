# P5 — Superflux (2017): Mitigation of Shock

## 📖 Citation (Harvard)
Superflux (2017) *Mitigation of Shock* [Speculative design installation]. London: Superflux. Available at: https://superflux.in/index.php/work/mitigation-of-shock/ (Accessed: 6 April 2026).

---

## What / So What / Now What

**What:** A fully realised speculative design installation depicting a 2050 apartment adapted to climate-change-induced food scarcity. The apartment contains a functioning vertical garden, fermentation jars, insect protein stores, and tools for radical food self-sufficiency. Visitors experience the installation as a visceral, **sensory encounter** with a possible future — including the smells of fermentation and the textures of food cultivation.

**So What:** Demonstrates the power of speculative design to make **abstract futures emotionally real**. Its use of food, smell, and tactile experience to communicate a future scenario is directly relevant to this project: dietary feedback is inherently sensory, and the most powerful design interventions may engage senses beyond the screen.

**Now What:** Use the installation format as inspiration for the speculative prototyping phase. Can feedback artefacts engage smell, texture, sound, or taste — not just screens? Connects to the concept of **ambient, non-numerical feedback** and to [[P4 - Dunne and Raby Placebo Project (2001)]] on non-digital feedback forms.

---

## 🔑 Key Qualities & Design Lessons

| Superflux Quality | Why It Matters | Application to This Project |
|------------------|---------------|----------------------------|
| **Full sensory immersion** | Makes the future visceral, not abstract | Dietary feedback artefacts could use smell, texture, warmth |
| **Working artefacts** | The garden grows; the fermentation works | Prototypes that are physically real, not just illustrated |
| **Food as protagonist** | Food is not data — it is the experience | Feedback system should centre food as pleasure, not fuel |
| **Future normalised** | 2050 feels like someone's Tuesday, not sci-fi | Mundane futures are more persuasive than spectacular ones |
| **Collective, not individual** | Food futures are social and political | Dietary feedback could be communal, not just personal |

| Overall Assessment | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Key Strength** | Shows speculative work can be emotionally and sensorially powerful, not just intellectual |
| **Key Limitation** | Food futures focus (sustainability/scarcity) differs from this project's phenomenology focus |
| **Design Lesson** | The most powerful dietary feedback might engage the body directly — not a screen |
