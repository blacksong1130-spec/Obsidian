## 3. Section 1: Introduction and Justification of the Topic (350 words)

I am interested in the future of preventive health behaviour, specifically the redesign of dietary feedback loops for individuals who seek to improve their nutrition but lack the sensory signals to track progress. This is a critical societal challenge because, unlike exercise or sleep, the benefits of good nutrition are almost entirely invisible on a human timescale. While a morning run is validated by Strava "kudos" and a night's sleep is distilled into an Oura "readiness score," a week of eating well produces no immediate legible signal. This is not a failure of willpower; it is a failure of feedback design.

Current dietary tools are fundamentally broken. Dominant platforms like MyFitnessPal rely on manual logging—a high-friction task that leads to a staggering abandonment rate. Data indicates that while ~26% of users engage on Day 1, this drops to a mere 3% by Day 30 (Business of Apps, 2024; Schembre et al., 2018). Applying the Fogg Behavior Model (B=MAP), it is evident that behavior occurs only when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge. Existing tools focus exclusively on motivation while neglecting "ability" by imposing high cognitive and manual burdens.

A Design Futures approach is essential here. This is not a simple UX problem; it is a systemic question regarding the relationship between technology and the intimate act of eating. By moving beyond the "manual log" toward emotionally resonant, speculative signals, this project interrogates how we can make metabolic health as meaningful as a social performance without descending into invasive surveillance. We must bridge the "Phenomenological Silence" of nutrition through design that respects "Food Freedom"—the right to make informed choices without guilt or compulsion (Tribole and Resch, 2020). This inquiry positions the designer not just as a tool-builder, but as a strategist navigating the intersection of biological reality, sensor maturation, and cultural shifts toward compassion-based health.

## 4. Section 2: Background—Literature, Practice, Signals, and Trends (1,150 words)

The primary barrier to health behaviour change is "Phenomenological Silence." When a user consumes a nutritious meal, the metabolic benefits—such as improved insulin sensitivity or reduced inflammation—occur at a cellular level that is imperceptible. This lack of immediate feedback allows "System 1" fast-thinking to prioritize immediate sensory rewards (taste/sugar) over abstract, long-term health benefits. This is deconstructed through the lens of "Hyperbolic Discounting," where the delay between action (eating a salad) and reward (longevity) results in the devaluation of the healthy choice in the present moment (Kahneman, 2011).

### Research Questions

1. Why does dietary behaviour produce so little immediate, legible feedback compared to exercise and sleep, and what are the design, behavioural, and physiological reasons for this gap?
2. What existing tools and approaches have attempted to address dietary feedback, and why do they fail to achieve sustained engagement?
3. What is the relationship between dietary feedback design and the risk of disordered eating, and how can feedback systems be designed to support food freedom?
4. What signals and speculative possibilities exist for alternative dietary feedback paradigms that are immediate, holistic, and emotionally resonant without being punitive?

### Evidence Synthesis: Market and Clinical Trends

The current landscape is defined by a tension between the booming "quantified self" market and the rising clinical risks of intensive tracking.

### Trend and Signal Mapping: The Futures Triangle

Using Inayatullah’s (2008) Futures Triangle (Figure 1), we can map the forces shaping the dietary feedback space:

- **The Pull:** The desire for "eating well to feel as rewarding as a run." This is driven by the maturation of Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM) and the promise of personalized metabolic feedback.
- **The Push:** Maturing sensor technology (Quantified Self 2.0) and the rise of "Anti-diet culture" which demands healthier psychological relationships with food.
- **The Weight:** The risk of eating disorders is a significant drag on innovation. Roth et al. (2024) established that intensive tracking is linked to elevated disordered eating symptoms. Additionally, the "food = fuel" narrative acts as a weight, stripping joy from eating and reducing it to a medicalized transaction.

### Critical Analysis of Current Tools

Existing practice is dominated by "manual entry" paradigms (e.g., MyFitnessPal, Noom). These tools assume that more data equals more change, yet they fail to account for the emotional tenor of the feedback. The feedback provided is often punitive—red bars for exceeding calories or guilt-inducing notifications. In contrast, emerging "metabolic health" platforms like Levels or Signos use CGMs to provide biological data, but they risk creating "data-induced anxiety" or over-indexing on glucose alone, ignoring the holistic "satisfaction factor" of food (Tribole and Resch, 2020).

### Transitioning to Problem Framing

The "Futures Wheel" (Figure 2) illustrates that if the feedback gap persists, we face a future where nutrition becomes further medicalized and health equity gaps widen, as only the wealthy access personalized metabolic data. This background research suggests a shift is required from "surveillance" to "resonance." We must move from tracking what we eat to feeling how we eat. The insights from the "French Paradox" and the Japanese concept of _Itadakimasu_ suggest that cultural ritual and embodiment provide more resilient feedback than digital scores (Rozin et al., 1999).

### Conclusion: Key Insights

1. **The Invisibility Gap:** Nutrition lacks the "social performance" of exercise, leading to behavioral abandonment.
2. **The Ethical Crisis:** Tracking technology is currently a double-edged sword, scaling disordered eating risks alongside health data.
3. **The Biological Opportunity:** Non-invasive sensors (CGMs) offer a technical path to visibility, but require a design "soul" to prevent surveillance risk.

## 5. Section 3: Problem Framing (350 words)

The central challenge is the **Intention-Action Gap**: the persistent failure to translate health intentions into sustained dietary habits due to the absence of perceivable success signals.

### Multi-Scalar Analysis

- **Systems:** This project intersects the **Human** (cognitive bias, emotional relationship with food), **Technology** (maturation of CGMs, AI food recognition), and **Society** (Diet culture vs. the Anti-Diet/Food Freedom movement). It recognizes that individual behavior is a product of these intersecting systems.
- **Temporal Scale:** A **5–20 year horizon (2035)** is utilized. This allows for the maturation of non-invasive sensors from medical to consumer-grade and reflects the time required for a cultural shift toward "Compassion-Based Design." Working within this scale enables speculative "backcasting"—working backward from a preferred future to identify today's intervention points (Martell, 2026).
- **Contextual Scale:** The focus is primarily **Micro**—the individual experience of a single meal or interaction with a feedback interface. By defining this boundary, the project avoids "scope creep" while maintaining the depth required to address the phenomenology of eating. However, it acknowledges the **Meso** scale of community food rituals (e.g., _Itadakimasu_) as inspiration for individual interventions.

These boundaries prevent the project from becoming a general public health campaign, instead focusing on the intimate interface between the body and the signal.

## 6. Section 4: Design Futures Approach (400 words)

The Design Futurist acts as an intermediary between biological reality and speculative possibility. This project adopts a framework built on four pillars: **Behaviour Design** (Fogg, Nudge), **Speculative Design** (Dunne & Raby), **Futures Studies** (Backcasting), and **Food Psychology** (Intuitive Eating).

### The Three Roles of the Strategist

1. **Cartographer of the Present:** Mapping the assumptions of current "log-heavy" tech and identifying why they fail.
2. **Critical Provocateur:** Designing artefacts that challenge the "surveillance" status quo and make visible the absences in health-tech.
3. **Preferential Futurist:** Using backcasting to identify how to reach a future where technology supports "Intuitive Eating" rather than overriding it.

### Methodological Framing: Compassion vs. Surveillance

We contrast "Compassion-Based Design" against "Surveillance Risks." Drawing on the **Intuitive Eating Principles** (Figure 3), the project treats these as hard design constraints: feedback must never override hunger signals, create "good/bad" food binaries, or weaponize emotional states (Tribole & Resch, 2020).

### Scenario Matrix: Navigating 2035

The **Scenario Matrix** (Figure 5) maps User Agency against Technology Type.

- **Intuitive Technology:** AI amplifies the body's own signals (Preferred Future).
- **Cultural/Social Ritual:** Focusing on environment and social prompts (e.g., Karl Krukow's COOP work).
- **High-Tech Biometric:** Individualized metabolic data designed with food freedom.
- **Surveillance Risk:** System control + High technology. This is the **"Future to Refuse"**—where dietary choices are governed by algorithms and punitive scoring.

## 7. Section 5: Methodology (550 words)

A **Dual-Track Methodology** is required for the "wicked problem" of dietary behaviour, balancing investigative rigor with generative speculation.

### Track 1: Investigative Research

- **Semi-structured Interviews:** 6–8 participants (Aged 22–45) to explore the lived experience of tracking. Interviews (45–60 mins) will focus on "emotional deserts" and why participants quit previous tools.
- **App Audit:** A systematic evaluation of 8 tools: **MyFitnessPal, Noom, Levels Health, Signos, Nutrisense, FoodVisor, NHS Weight Loss Plan, and Strava** (as a cross-disciplinary benchmark). Each will be audited for "Feedback Immediacy" and "Emotional Tenor."
- **Autoethnographic Diary:** A 4-week self-study adopting an "observer" perspective. I will log not just "what" was eaten, but the "Phenomenological Response"—noticing hunger, fullness, and social context without judgment.

### Track 2: Generative Research

- **Scenario Building:** Constructing three 2035 scenarios based on the Scenario Matrix.
- **Speculative Prototyping:** Creating 3–5 artefacts. These will include:
    - An **ambient object** that changes texture/temperature to reflect metabolic balance.
    - A **social ritual tool** inspired by _Itadakimasu_ that facilitates non-digital feedback.
- **Participatory Critique:** Sharing these artefacts with interview participants to test for "resonance" vs. "anxiety."

### Data Protocols

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Data Type|Source|Recruitment / Criteria|
|**Primary Qualitative**|Interviews / 4-week Diaries|Adults 22-45; Prior tracking use; **Excluding active ED.**|
|**Secondary Qualitative**|Lit Review / 8-App Audit|Academic databases; App stores; Design archives.|

### Methodological Justification

This methodology is superior to quantitative tracking because it captures the **Phenomenology of Eating**. By using reflexive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006), the project ensures the "Dietary Wellbeing Framework" is derived from user experience, not just biometric data.

## 12. Appendix C: Ethics Checklist

**Primary Ethical Safeguard:**

- **Exclusion Criteria:** Any participant with a current diagnosis of an eating disorder will be excluded from the primary research phase (interviews and diaries) to prevent potential harm or trigger.
- **Compassion-Based Design:** All interventions will be tested against the "Intuitive Eating" framework to ensure they do not create "good/bad food" binaries or promote body-shaming.
- **Data Privacy:** All participant data will be anonymized, and no biometric data will be stored without explicit, time-limited consent.
