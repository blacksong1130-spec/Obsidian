## Slide-by-Slide Talking Guide

---

### SLIDE 1 — The Opening Provocation

**Visual**: Full-bleed slide. Just one line of text, large.

> **"What if eating well felt like something?"**

**What you say:**

> "Every morning, after a run — your body tells you. After a good night's sleep — your watch tells you. But after three weeks of eating well? Nothing. Silence. You have no idea if it's working.
>
> That's not a motivation problem. That's a feedback problem. And that's what this project is about."

**Why this works**: You open with the *felt experience* of the problem, not a slide of background research. Tutors are immediately curious.

---

### SLIDE 2 — The Commercial Evidence

**Visual**: Three numbers, large. Plus the key quote.

> **3–4%** Day-30 retention for health and fitness apps
> **$25 billion** annual US investment in digital health
> **73%** of MyFitnessPal users with an eating disorder say the app contributed to their condition

**What you say:**

> "This is not an academic problem. The health tech industry is spending $25 billion a year in the US alone — and producing 3% retention rates. The dominant feedback paradigm — manual logging, calorie counting — is demonstrably failing at scale.
>
> And for a significant number of users, it's not just failing. It's causing harm. 73% of MyFitnessPal users with an eating disorder say the app contributed to their condition. That's not a bug. That's what happens when you design feedback to judge rather than reflect."

**Why this works**: You've established both the commercial opportunity and the ethical stakes in 90 seconds.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Why Now: The Three Signals

**Visual**: A simple timeline with three marked moments.

> **June 2024** → Abbott Lingo receives FDA OTC clearance. First CGM for non-diabetics hits Walgreens retail.
> **2023** → *Outlive* by Peter Attia sells 1.5 million copies. Medicine 3.0 enters mainstream culture.
> **2024** → UK eating disorder rates in 17–19 year olds: 12.5%, up from 0.8% in 2017. A 15-fold increase.

**What you say:**

> "Three things happened at the same time. The technology crossed the consumer threshold. The cultural appetite for proactive health thinking reached mass scale. And the harm of punitive dietary tools became undeniable.
>
> The timing for this research isn't arbitrary. It's specific. This is the moment the design question becomes commercially urgent."

---

### SLIDE 4 — The Design White Space

**Visual**: A 2×2 or simple Venn diagram.

> **The white space**: Bioindividual measurement × Non-punitive design × Behaviour architecture × Food Freedom

> **Who's in the market now?**
> - Abbott Lingo: sensor engineers. No design philosophy.
> - Levels Health: biometric dashboard. No compassion framework.
> - MyFitnessPal: 73% ED harm rate. Punitive by design.
> - Noom: weight loss framing. Inherently moralistic.

**What you say:**

> "Every major player entering the Medicine 3.0 dietary space is doing so with an engineering mindset, not a design mindset. They have sensors. They have data. What they don't have is an answer to the question this project asks: *how should feedback feel?* What is the right affective register for dietary data?
>
> That's a design research problem. And it's unoccupied."

---

### SLIDE 5 — Medicine 2.0 → 3.0 Timeline

**Visual**: A horizontal timeline — the one from Section 2 of the IRP. Visual, not text-heavy.

Key moments marked:
- 2015: Zeevi et al. (Cell) — bioindividuality proven at scale
- 2019: Levels Health founded
- 2023: *Outlive* published
- 2024: Abbott Lingo OTC → **Paradigm shifts**
- **2026: This project** ←
- 2035: Speculative horizon

**What you say:**

> "Medicine 2.0 is the system we all grew up in: wait for symptoms, then treat. Its dietary guidance assumes a standardised human body — food pyramids, generic calorie targets.
>
> Medicine 3.0 is emerging now: proactive, personalised, continuous. And a 2015 study in Cell is the scientific pivot point. 800 people, 46,898 meals — identical foods produce dramatically different responses in different bodies. The individual baseline is the only meaningful baseline.
>
> This project sits *at the design interface of that transition*. 2026 is when design research can define what Medicine 3.0 dietary feedback feels like, before the engineers get there and make it worse."

---

### SLIDE 6 — Three Research Questions

**Visual**: Three numbered questions, clean and minimal.

> 1. **Technical**: How might micro-scale, non-invasive sensing reduce reliance on manual food logging and make internal dietary processing legible for non-specialists?
>
> 2. **Architectural**: How might the eating environment be redesigned to provide low-friction prompts and non-punitive feedback that supports healthier decisions?
>
> 3. **Ethical**: What feedback formats can support non-punitive reflection, minimise anxiety, and avoid reinforcing disordered eating — while protecting Food Freedom?

**What you say:**

> "Three questions. One empirical — can we make the body legible without surveillance? One spatial — can the environment do work that willpower can't? And one ethical — how do we know when feedback helps and when it harms?
>
> The third question is the constraint that shapes everything else."

### SLIDE 7 — Methodology: Dual-Track

**Visual**: Two parallel tracks — visual diagram.

```
Track 1: INVESTIGATIVE          Track 2: GENERATIVE
─────────────────────           ─────────────────────
Phase 0: Peer conversations →   ← Speculative artefacts
Semi-structured interviews  →   ← Scenario building
Autoethnographic diary      →   ← Participatory critique
App audit (8 tools)         →   ← Design principles output
```

**What you say:**

> "Two parallel tracks that feed each other. The investigative track generates empirical data on lived experience — what does the feedback gap actually feel like for real people? The generative track produces artefacts that make alternative futures concrete — physical things that can be tested, critiqued, and thrown away.
>
> Phase 0 peer conversations are already starting this week. The first artefact concept is what I'll show you now."

### SLIDE 8 — FIRST SPECULATIVE ARTEFACT: "The After Glow"

**Visual**: This is the most important slide. Options:
- A sketch or rendering of the ceramic disc
- A photograph of a ceramic object with a warm amber glow
- A storyboard: person eating → picks up disc → 60 seconds → release
- If you've made a rough mock-up: show it physically

**What you say:**

> "Every health app closes its loop before the meal: plan what you eat, log it before, track the calorie budget. The moment of eating itself is invisible to these systems.
>
> The After Glow asks a different question. What if the loop closed *after* the meal — in your hands, not on your screen?
>
> This is a ceramic disc that sits on your dining table. After you eat, you pick it up for 60 seconds. It connects to a wearable biosensor — and responds with subtle temperature and soft light. Warmth when your body is settled. Soft coolness when it's actively working. No number. No score. No comparison.
>
> The sensation is: *your body, right now, after this meal.*
>
> It draws from Japanese *Gochisōsama* — the closing ritual of a meal. From Ayurvedic post-meal observation — which Zeevi et al. (2015) has now proven scientifically necessary. And from the French habit of simply... sitting after eating.
>
> This is a design probe, not a product. It's here to ask: can sensation replace data as the primary feedback medium? And does it create a different emotional relationship with eating than a dashboard does?"

**At this point, if you have a physical mock-up: hand it to the tutors.**

### SLIDE 9 — The Non-Punitive Design Framework

**Visual**: The Five Attributes table — clean, spare.

| Attribute | Punitive Example | Non-Punitive Principle |
|---|---|---|
| Descriptive, not evaluative | "You're over your daily target." | Describe what happened; don't render a verdict |
| Relative, not absolute | Compared to dietary guidelines | Compared only to your own baseline |
| Closed-loop | The diary is always incomplete | Every interaction closes |
| Sensation-first | Numbers as primary interface | Sensation/metaphor first; numbers available by choice |
| Optional | Streak penalty for non-use | Zero consequence for not engaging |

**What you say:**

> "The phrase 'reflect, not judge' appears throughout this project — but I realised I'd never actually defined what that means at the interface level. What does non-punitive feedback actually look like as a design decision?
>
> These five attributes are the answer. They're not philosophical positions — they're evaluable criteria. Any UX team, any product, any design crit: you can run these five questions and know whether what you're looking at qualifies.
>
> And they become a commercial output: an audit tool that health-tech companies can use to certify their products as non-punitive. The Nielsen Norman Group built a $40 million consultancy on UX heuristics. This is the same model — for dietary feedback."

### SLIDE 11 — Open Questions for Crit

**Visual**: Three questions, large, open.

> 1. **The sensation question**: Can a physical, embodied sensation — warmth, coolness, light — replace a number as meaningful dietary feedback? Or does this just feel vague?
>
> 2. **The silence question**: The After Glow has no memory, no trend, no comparison. Is that a feature or a fatal flaw?
>
> 3. **The design boundary**: Where does "reflect, not judge" become "so gentle it's useless"? Where is the minimum threshold of legibility?

**What you say:**

> "I'm not here to defend this artefact. I'm here to find out where it fails.
>
> Three questions I'd genuinely like the room's help on — and I'll write down whatever you say."

## Preparation Checklist (Do Before the Crit)

- [ ] Have you done at least **one Phase 0 peer conversation**? If so, have at least **one real direct quote** ready to use in Slide 8 or 9. Real participant language is worth ten slides of research.
- [ ] Do you have a **physical representation** of The After Glow? Even a cardboard disc the right size, with a warm light behind it. The physical object changes the room.
- [ ] Have you **timed** your run-through? Target 12 minutes, leave 3 for questions to open.
- [ ] Do you have your **two or three strongest citations** ready verbally? Zeevi et al. (2015) for the bioindividuality claim. Business of Apps (2026) for the retention rate. Roth et al. (2024) for the ED harm claim.
- [ ] Is there a **simple one-sentence answer** ready if a tutor asks: "But what IS this, specifically?" → "It's a design probe testing whether embodied sensation can replace visual data as a dietary feedback medium — using post-meal metabolic response."

## If Tutors Push Back

**"This is too vague — what does 'non-punitive' actually mean?"**
→ Show Slide 9 — the five-attribute framework is your answer. Say: "I spent significant time this week defining exactly that, because you were right — it was a philosophy without a specification. Here's the specification."

**"What makes you think people want sensation instead of data?"**
→ Say: "I don't know yet — that's what Phase 0 is testing. The artefact is designed to generate that question in real conversations. I'll have real participant responses to bring to the next crit."

**"How is this different from what Levels Health is already doing?"**
→ Say: "Levels Health is a biometric dashboard with a score. It's sensation-last and evaluation-first. Structurally, it fails four of the five non-punitive attributes. The white space isn't the sensor technology — it's the design philosophy."

**"You still haven't done any real user research."**
→ Acknowledge it directly: "You're right — this project is still pre-empirical. Phase 0 starts this week. What I can commit to for the next crit is real participant quotes and a physical mock-up of the artefact."
