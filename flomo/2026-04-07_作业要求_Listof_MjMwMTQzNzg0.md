#irp/作业要求

List of IRP Proposal Tasks between 18th Feb-24th March
Completion requirements

-
Visual Annotated Literature and Practice Review (Submitted to Moodle as a PDF or link to a digital file, Deadline: 24th March, 09:00)
This review is designed to help you:

Build a growing collection of sources that will inform your entire project and future interests

Become familiar with your topic and understand how others are thinking about it, helping you identify the unique contribution of your own project

Find inspiration in related work by designers and others, supporting decisions about your methodology and approach

Position your practice within a broader, transdisciplinary design context

Instructions

-
Choose a visual medium

Select a physical or digital visual format to organise, connect, and reflect on your sources (e.g. board, map, canvas).

-
Gather sources

Identify a minimum of:

20 literature sources, and

20 practice-based projects relevant to your topic

Literature

Draw from design and other disciplines

Use frameworks such as PESTEL, STEEP, or similar to ensure a holistic view

Reflect on the past, present, and future of your topic to identify drivers, trends, and signals

Literature must include at least 5 different types of credible sources, such as:

Academic papers

White papers

Trend reports

Talks or lectures

Documentaries

Mainstream or popular media

Practice

Include at least 5 different types of practice-based sources, for example:

Projects, exhibitions, artifacts

Materials, products, services

Performances, songs, films, fiction

Scientific, government, or community initiatives

Projects can be historical or contemporary, successful or failed, speculative or fictional

Look broadly: designers, creatives, futurists, scientists, institutions, and beyond

Ask questions constantly. Research is not about knowing what you will find, but about learning what to ask next. Start with what you need to know, then let each discovery guide your next question.

-
Thematic grouping

Group your sources into themes and subthemes

Clearly show connections and relationships between sources

Consider what different sources have in common and how they speak to one another

-
Annotations

Select:

10 most relevant literature sources, and

10 most relevant practice projects

For each, write approximately 100 words (keywords and bullet points are fine), addressing:

What: Key points relevant to your topic; who created it and their background

So what: Why this source matters; its significance to your project; your critical reflections

Now what: How you might use this source; connections to other sources; new questions it raises

-
Document your research journey

Keep notes and photographs of your research process

Record how one discovery led to another (e.g. exhibition → material → biodesign → new question → new source)

Bring this documentation to your tutorials

This material will support assessment, reflection on methodology, and your portfolio

**Please note that insights from your literature review and practice review will go in your IRP proposal report later on, so it is advisable to keep documentation in order so that translation to the report would be less of a task.

-
Reflection

Be prepared to discuss and reflect on your review during the week of 24 March.

Research Questions and Conclusion (submitted with Visual Annotated Literature and Practice Review)
As part of your visual annotated literature and practice review, you must include a short section that clearly articulates the research questions guiding your secondary research and a concluding synthesis of your findings.

Research Question(s)
Clearly state the key research question or questions you sought to answer through your secondary research

These questions should reflect what you needed to understand in order to develop your project proposal

Research questions may evolve as your research progresses; this section can reflect that development

Conclusion
Your review should end with a concise conclusion that synthesises your findings, including:

Three most important trends, drivers, or emerging futures relevant to your topic

Key gaps in current knowledge and practice that your project could respond to or explore further

This section should demonstrate your ability to move from gathering sources to making sense of them, showing critical reflection, pattern recognition, and an understanding of where your work sits within existing discourse and practice.

Problem Framing (Submitted with Visual Annotated Literature and Practice Review)
As part of your visual annotated literature and practice review, you must include a short section to clearly define the problem space your project will engage with. Drawing on insights from your literature and practice review, articulate the challenge you want to investigate and explain why it matters. See the Problem Framing Cards from Research Methodologies here.

The Challenge
Describe the social and/or environmental challenge you wish to explore

Explain why this challenge is important, urgent, or under-examined

Situate the challenge within a broader context, referencing insights from your research where relevant

System-Level Perspective(s)
Identify the system or systems you are focusing on, such as:

Human

Societal

Environmental / ecological

Technological

Explain why these system-level perspectives are relevant to your challenge

You may focus on one system or explore the interactions between multiple systems

Temporal Scale
Specify the time horizon you are working within, for example:

Short term

Medium term

Long term

Very long term / century-scale

Explain why this temporal scale is appropriate for your topic and what it allows you to examine

Contextual Scale
Define the scale of intervention or observation, such as:

Macro (global, national, systemic)

Meso (institutional, community, organisational)

Micro (individual, material, experiential)

Justify why this scale is relevant and how it shapes your framing of the problem

This section should demonstrate your ability to frame complexity, make informed choices about scope, and articulate the boundaries of your project. Clear writing, structured paragraphs, and critical reflection are more important than definitive answers

Three-Dimensional Object Representing Your Topic (To bring along during IRP proposal tutorial on 25th March)
You are required to identify or create a three-dimensional, tangible object that represents your research topic and helps communicate your problem framing.

Object Description
Locate or develop a physical object that meaningfully represents your theme

This may be:

An existing object

A modified object

A newly created artefact

The object can take many forms, such as a sketch model, product, artefact, sculpture, material sample, or prototype

Purpose
The object should help illustrate the issue, tension, or question at the centre of your project

It does not need to propose a solution; it can simply make the problem visible or tangible

Example
For instance, if your topic relates to textile waste, you might bring a damaged or discarded T-shirt to represent material degradation, overproduction, or disposal practices.

This object will act as a conversation tool during tutorials and critiques, supporting discussion of your topic, framing, and emerging direction.
