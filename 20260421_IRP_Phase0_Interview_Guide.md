> **How to use this guide**
>
> This is a complete protocol for Phase 0 peer conversations. Phase 0 conversations are **exploratory and informal** — they happen before formal interviews. The goal is to listen, identify unexpected themes, and practise your role as a researcher.
>
> - Duration: 30–45 minutes (peer conversations), or 45–60 minutes (formal semi-structured interviews)
> - Recording: Ask permission first. If declined, take brief notes immediately after.
> - Language: Avoid the words "good," "bad," "healthy," "unhealthy," "diet." Use "eating differently" or "how you eat."
> - Your job: Listen more than you speak. Follow threads. Sit with silence.

---

## Pre-Interview Setup Checklist

- [ ] Have you explained the project purpose in one sentence? *(Sample: "I'm researching why it's so hard to feel like eating well is making a difference — and whether the tools we use help or make it worse.")*
- [ ] Have you confirmed recording consent (if recording)?
- [ ] Have you explained they can stop or skip any question at any time?
- [ ] Are you in a private or quiet space where they feel comfortable?
- [ ] Do you have a notepad for key phrases (even if recording)?

## Section 1: Warm-Up — Establishing Comfort (5–8 minutes)

*Purpose: Lower the participant's guard. Establish that this is not a judgemental conversation. Build rapport.*

**Opening frame (say this before questions):**

> "I want to start by being clear: there are no right answers here, and I'm not interested in whether you eat 'well' by any particular standard. I'm interested in your actual experience — what it's like to be in your body, making food decisions, day to day. You're the expert on that."

**Warm-up questions:**

1. "What did you eat today, or most recently? Don't worry about what it was — I'm just curious how easy or hard it is to remember."
   *(Observe: Do they remember easily or not? Do they immediately apologise or qualify what they ate?)*

2. "When you wake up in the morning, do you usually have a sense of how you want to eat that day — or does it just happen?"

3. "Is there a place — a kitchen, a restaurant, a spot — where eating feels most comfortable or most like yourself?"

---

## Section 2: The Relationship with Food Tracking (10–12 minutes)

*Purpose: Understand their direct experience with dietary apps and tools — without leading them toward positive or negative framing.*

4. "Have you ever used an app or tool to track what you eat? Tell me about that — what made you start?"
   - *If yes:* "What was it like in the first week? And what happened over time?"
   - *If no:* "Have you ever considered it, or actively decided not to? What was behind that?"

5. "When you were using it *(or: imagining using it)*, what did the data actually do for you? Did it change what you ate the next meal?"
   *(Probe: "Was that change how you expected? Did it feel good, neutral, or —?")*

6. "Was there a specific moment where the app or tool made you feel something strongly — either in a good direction or not?"
   *(Listen for: shame, pride, anxiety, relief, indifference. Don't suggest the emotion — wait for their word.)*

7. "Why did you stop? / Why have you kept using it?"
   *(Key probe: What specifically triggered stopping? One bad day? Gradual fade? Specific data point?)*

---

## Section 3: The Feedback Gap — What Does Eating Well Feel Like? (10–12 minutes)

*Purpose: Explore the core research problem — phenomenological silence — without naming it. Let participants locate it themselves.*

8. "When you think about 'eating well' — whatever that means to you — what does it feel like in your body? Not what it means intellectually — what does it *feel* like?"
   *(Probe if needed: "Is there a sensation? Or nothing? Or something delayed?")*
   *(This is the core question. Give it space. Don't rescue them from the silence if they pause.)*

9. "Can you think of a time when you ate in a way that you felt was 'right' for you — and then thought: did that actually do anything? Or could you feel it?"

10. "And by contrast — have you ever eaten something that you immediately felt in your body? What was that like — and why do you think that one registered differently?"

11. "Between exercise, sleep, and eating — which one gives you the clearest signal that you did something good for yourself? Why do you think that is?"
    *(Observe: Almost everyone will say exercise or sleep. The absence of "eating" is the data.)*

---

## Section 4: Environment and Micro-Context (8–10 minutes)

*Purpose: Explore the "eating environment" — where choice architecture and design can intervene.*

12. "Where do most of your meals happen? Describe a typical eating environment — what's around you, who's there, how much time do you have?"

13. "Is there anything in your eating environment — your kitchen layout, your fridge, your social situation — that makes eating the way you want to feel easier? Harder?"

14. "Think about the last meal you ate that felt completely on autopilot. What pulled you toward it? Was there a prompt — something you saw, something someone said, a time pressure?"

15. "If you could change one thing about your physical environment that would change how you eat — without using willpower or knowledge — what would it be?"

---

## Section 5: Imagining Different Feedback (8–10 minutes)

*Purpose: Open space for speculative thinking. Introduce the "after the meal" concept without describing The After Glow directly. Gauge intuitive response.*

16. "If your body could somehow tell you — in the hour after you ate — something useful about that meal, what would you want to know? And how would you want to receive it?"
    *(Probe: "As a number? A feeling? A visual? Something else?")*

17. "Here's a hypothetical: imagine a tool that tells you nothing before the meal — no logging, no planning. But after the meal, for 60 seconds, it shows you something about how your body responded. Would that be interesting or anxiety-provoking to you?"
    *(This probes the core Before vs. After distinction of the IRP.)*

18. "What would make you *trust* a feedback signal around food? And what would immediately make you distrust it?"

19. "Is there anything you would *not* want to know about how your body responds to food? Are there limits to how much information feels useful — and where does it start feeling like surveillance or judgement?"

---

## Section 6: Closing — Reflections and Research Framing (5 minutes)

*Purpose: Bring the conversation to a clean close, check for discomfort, and identify any themes you missed.*

20. "If you could redesign your relationship with eating — not what you eat, but the *feeling* of eating — what would be different?"

21. "Is there anything about your relationship with food that you've never really been asked about in any of these conversations?"

**Closing statement:**

> "Thank you so much — this has been really useful. I just want to check: was anything in that conversation uncomfortable, or is there anything you'd like to take back? Also — is there anyone you know whose relationship with food or tracking is really different from yours, who might be willing to talk to me?"

## During and After the Conversation: Note-Taking Protocol

### During (only if not recording, or as supplement):

Mark these in your notes:
- **Q** = a quote you want to capture verbatim (exact words)
- **T** = a theme that seems to be emerging
- **!** = something surprising, unexpected, or contradicting your assumption
- **?** = something you want to follow up on later

### Immediately after (within 30 minutes):

Write a one-page synthesis:

1. **Three most surprising things they said**
2. **The moment they seemed most emotionally activated** (which question? what were the words?)
3. **What question they struggled to answer** (absence = data)
4. **One direct quote I want to keep** (copy it exactly)
5. **Does this change anything about the research design?**

## Questions to Avoid (and Why)

| Avoid | Why | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Do you eat healthily?" | Triggers self-justification and shame | "What does eating in a way that feels right to you actually look like?" |
| "What's your diet?" | Associates with weight-loss framing | "How do you tend to eat day-to-day?" |
| "Does the app help you lose weight?" | Outcome-focused, punitive frame | "Does the app change how you feel about eating?" |
| "Is that food bad for you?" | Introduces moral judgement | Avoid entirely |
| "You should try..." | You are not a health coach | Stay curious; no advice |

---

## Ethical Reminders

- If anyone mentions a current eating disorder or active clinical treatment: **thank them for sharing, do not probe further on food tracking specifically, redirect to broader "relationship with body" framing**
- No participant should leave the conversation feeling judged about their eating
- If a conversation becomes emotionally heavy: "Would you like to take a break, or shall we stop here?"
